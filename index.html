Biography

I am an Electrical Engineering undergraduate at the University of Calgary (Digital Engineering minor), working at the intersection of machine learning and resource-constrained hardware. My research studies how learned representations behave under distribution shift and how reliable models can be deployed on the edge — building systems that know what they don't know.

My core line of work compares supervised and self-supervised representation learning (SimCLR, SupCon, BYOL, ViT) for out-of-distribution robustness, showing that self-supervised representations degrade more gracefully under domain shift and support early-exit inference that skips ~83.5% of samples at maintained accuracy. This work grounded an edge-deployed corrosion-detection system that uses cosine-similarity prototype matching for unknown-class rejection (95.6% accuracy, 0.873 AUROC).

Alongside ML, I work on the systems these models run on: autonomous rovers integrating LiDAR, IMU, and encoders with edge inference on Jetson and Raspberry Pi; RTL design in SystemVerilog on Artix-7 FPGAs; and analog IC design in Cadence Virtuoso with a TSMC PDK. I previously held an R&D internship in semiconductor device characterization at Lision Technology (Taiwan).

My longer-term research goal is to develop machine-learning systems that are both scientifically interpretable and deployable under real physical constraints.

Transformer Language Model from Scratch

  • Developed a decoder-only Transformer language model from scratch using PyTorch, without relying on built-in Transformer modules or high-level training frameworks.
  • Implemented multi-head causal self-attention, residual connections, pre-normalization, RMSNorm, SwiGLU feed-forward layers, and Rotary Positional Embeddings.
  • Built a byte-level Byte Pair Encoding tokenizer supporting learned merge operations, special-token handling, text pre-tokenization, encoding, and UTF-8 decoding.
  • Implemented core neural-network operations, including linear and embedding layers, numerically stable softmax, scaled dot-product attention, and cross-entropy loss.
  • Created a custom AdamW optimizer with bias-corrected moment estimates, decoupled weight decay, cosine learning-rate decay, linear warm-up, and gradient clipping.
  • Designed a next-token prediction training pipeline with random sequence batching, configurable hyperparameters, checkpoint restoration, and training-loss logging.
  • Used memory-mapped NumPy datasets to efficiently train on tokenized corpora without loading the complete dataset into memory.
  • Enabled hardware-accelerated model training on Apple Silicon through PyTorch Metal Performance Shaders support.

Positional Encoding Ablation for Neural Machine Translation

Controlled head-to-head comparison of LSTM, sinusoidal Transformer, and RoPE Transformer on English→Portuguese and English→Mandarin

+0.031ROUGE-1 vs sinusoidal
0.6817RoPE ROUGE-1 (En→Pt)
0 extraparameters
2language pairs
PyTorch RoPE Transformer seq2seq LSTM baseline ROUGE-1 MPS / M1
  • Implemented RoPE from scratch (half-split convention with cached cos/sin buffers) inside custom encoder/decoder layers mirroring the PyTorch nn.Transformer API, applied to Q/K in self-attention only and intentionally omitted from cross-attention.
  • Matched the sinusoidal and RoPE Transformers on width, depth, FFN size, head count, and parameter budget so the only intended difference was the positional encoding scheme.
  • Built a shared training and evaluation harness (linear LR warmup, AdamW, gradient clipping, masked NLL, early stopping with best-checkpoint restore) reused across all three models for a fair comparison.
  • Observed RoPE improving ROUGE-1 from 0.6505 to 0.6817 on English→Portuguese (200 validation pairs, greedy decoding, single seed) at identical parameter count.
  • Identified a clear qualitative failure mode on character-level Mandarin: the sinusoidal model produced repetition loops (e.g. "哥哥哥哥…") that RoPE largely avoided, indicating better positional behavior on long, structurally distant outputs.
  • Documented limitations explicitly — single seed, ROUGE-1 only, small corpus, no KV cache during decoding — to keep the claim scoped to what the experiment actually supports.
